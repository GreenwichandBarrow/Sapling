## Notes

### Software Market State

The software M&A market is very murky right now. Deals signed in Jan/Feb 2026 closed at 2025 valuations, but new deals are being retraded. Volume has slowed significantly.

Flight to quality is more pronounced than ever. There is a clear bifurcation: mediocre deals get mid-single-digit ARR multiples, while very good deals still command double-digit multiples.

"No man's land" exists for companies with 15-20% growth and 10-15% EBITDA margins. Those deals are not getting done.

### Riverside's Approach

[[entities/riverside|Riverside]] manages $13B AUM across 7 fund families. [[entities/jake-stoller|Jake Stoller]]'s fund is $1.9B, targeting companies under $10M EBITDA. 95% of their deal flow falls into three categories: traditional SaaS, business services, and franchising.

They are now focusing exclusively on vertical SaaS, industry agnostic. Their thesis: small TAM is no longer a negative. It means less competitive pressure from AI entrants who target large horizontal markets.

Starting criteria: 5-15M ARR, breakeven or slightly burning, 20%+ growth.

### AI Impact on Software Acquisitions

12 months ago, buyers needed to see an AI roadmap. Today, buyers need to see AI deployed in the product (not necessarily monetized yet). For sellers not planning to exit this year, the advice is to start building AI as a revenue line.

The software development lifecycle is changing fundamentally with agents and shorter development loops. Buyers are monitoring AI adoption rates among target companies' dev teams.

### What Makes Software Defensible Now

Key defensibility signals in the current market:

- **Control point of the customer** -- the company IS the system of record, not a module feeding data to an ERP
- **Compliance and regulatory driven** -- mandated usage creates switching costs
- **High cost of failure** -- govtech, healthcare, investigations. Slow AI adoption curve because mistakes are catastrophic
- **Domain expertise as moat** -- deep industry knowledge embedded in product
- **Long sales cycles now viewed as POSITIVE** -- they are a barrier to disruption from AI-native entrants
- **Reference-driven customer bases** -- word of mouth markets are harder to penetrate
- **Data opportunities** -- proprietary data that improves with usage

### Customer Concentration Warning

Top 3 customers exceeding 30% of revenue is the biggest negative signal in the BK Growth portfolio companies. There is a shockingly high rate of key customers leaving within 12-24 months post-acquisition. The information asymmetry is basically impossible to diligence.

Jake's example: 31% concentration in one deal, COO told him "if this goes, it's your ass."

### Pricing Model Evolution

Service Titan switched from per-seat to a percentage-of-revenue model (2% of customer revenue). Pushback from pest control and landscaping operators who lose operating leverage as they grow. The market is shifting: module expansion matters more than user count expansion now. Per-seat pricing is under pressure as employment bases shrink due to AI.

### Debt Markets

Rates have moved out, leverage down approximately 1-1.5 turns from the peak. Lower middle market is somewhat insulated from the worst of it. Big LBOs done at high ARR multiples are now overleveraged versus current market pricing.

### Adam's Exit Learnings (Assisted Living Software)

Adam recently exited his assisted living software company to Level Equity. Key takeaways:

- **Platform > product > feature** for AI insulation. Being a platform is the strongest defensive position.
- Mission critical with catastrophic failure consequences = defensible
- Core system of record with 60+ integrations proved centrality to buyers
- AI came up with every single buyer in the process
- Three lenses every buyer used: risk to the end market from AI, risk of the product being replaced by AI, and what the company is doing with AI
- Weakness on AI adoption in the dev lifecycle was a ding on valuation
