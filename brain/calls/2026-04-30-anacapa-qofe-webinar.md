## Context

Educational webinar from Anacapa Beacon series. Mike (Hollywell founder, ex-PWC large-deal director, Numera now post-Hollywell exit) walked searchers through the Q of E (Quality of Earnings) / financial due diligence process — what it is, when to engage, what's in the report, common red/yellow flags, and how working capital negotiations get botched.

## Notes

### What Q of E actually is
- Marketing term for financial due diligence (buy-side or sell-side).
- **NOT regulated** by AICPA or any audit standard — wide quality variance across providers (Big Four down to "bookkeepers marketing on Twitter").
- Always demand a **substantiated procedure list** in the engagement letter before paying.
- "Q of E full" vs "Q of E light" — same procedures; light is just smaller deals (saves fees because there's less to look at, not less rigor).

### When to engage
- After meaningful LOI accepted, not before.
- Phase 1 typically takes 4-5 weeks engagement-to-initial-call-with-seller.
- Hollywell broken-deal commitment: **if Phase 1 turns up a yellow/red flag and you walk away, no Phase 2 invoice** — search-fund Q of E typically $20K total.

### Deliverable
- Long Excel workbook (NOT shiny PDF — searchers want the model inputs).
- Includes cash proof, P&L workbook, working-capital dig, EBITDA bridge, free-cash-flow bridge.
- Walk-through call to interpret yellow/red flags before deciding on Phase 2.

### Red flags Mike covered (high-value reference)

**Add-back nonsense (most common on small-deal SIMs):**
- "Fire 30% of staff Day 1" — if true, why hasn't owner already done it?
- "Don't pay annual bonuses" — kills retention of operators you depend on.
- Owner's boat / private jet / family travel — legit add-backs IF non-recurring and not part of operations.

**Revenue-side fraud signals:**
- AR spike paired with revenue spike right before sale — usually channel stuffing or accelerated recognition.
- Cash-proof equation: opening AR + revenue – cash applied = ending AR. If math doesn't work or bank statements look edited (different fonts in PDF) → fraud, walk away.

**Off-balance-sheet items:**
- Lawsuits, insurance issues, undeclared debt — surface via UCC checks + secretary inquiries.
- **Personal background checks on owners** — Numera example: 60-year-old CPA with recent bar fight + missed alimony/child support → killed the deal at last stage.

**Unreported expenses (the killer):**
- Cash payments under the table for labor — example: commercial landscaping in Ohio, surprise site visit found 30-35 workers, books showed 15. Owner stonewalled, Hollywell waited them out 6 weeks until they confessed → buyer walked away from a profitable deal.
- Industry tells: payments to nameless vendors, cash-out transactions with no offsetting expense.

**Related-party transactions:**
- Texas dry-cleaning roll-up: owners owned the real estate. Hollywell pushed for **lease negotiation contemporaneous with close**. Buyer didn't, trusted seller — rents jacked post-close, killed EBITDA 1:1.

### Working capital — most-mishandled concept (CRITICAL)
- Definition: current assets – current liabilities, **strip out cash** (sellers walk away with cash on cash-free/debt-free deal).
- Positive working capital = day-1 float requirement on the buyer.
- **Negative working capital = deposit-based business** = AWESOME for buyers BUT only if Q of E provider correctly classifies deposits as **deferred revenue (liability), not receivables**.
- Window-washing example (Orlando search fund): 90-120 day commercial AR offset by deposits. Deal fatigue → buy-side attorney let seller take all cash. Buyer spent first 6-8 months in the hole covering revenue not yet earned. Seller had no contractual obligation to return.
- Mike's preferred peg: **trailing 12-month average**. Some PE firms over-engineer with 30/60/90-day pegs and post-close trueups; keep it simple if you can.
- Banks will **finance working-capital line if you can demonstrate the need** — use the Q of E table to support that ask.

### EBITDA reality
- Mike's data across hundreds of Q of Es: only ~2 deals ever came out **higher** than the SIM showed (1 tied, hundreds came out lower).
- Build buy-price floor/ceiling **before** Q of E lands so you're not negotiating against your own emotion.
- Average year-1 post-close EBITDA drop: **~10%** (Sarah's Anacapa research). Factor into covenant cushion. Anacapa runs slightly more conservative leverage to protect against this.

### Asset-intensive caveat
- For service businesses with truck/equipment fleet (HVAC, landscaping, etc.), check **deferred R&M** — owners often skip maintenance final 1-2 years before sale.
- Not an EBITDA hit (D&A is carved out) but a free-cash-flow hit. Always look at FCF alongside EBITDA, not in silo.

### Search-specific add-backs Sarah called out
- New CEO salary (replacing owner who under-paid themselves to take cash elsewhere).
- Sales hire / GM hire required to hit modeled growth.
- Insurance under-coverage gap.
- Distribution-center upgrade if growth plan requires it.

### Industries Hollywell does NOT touch
- Healthcare with payor systems / true medical practices (referral partner instead).
- Everything else industry-agnostic.
