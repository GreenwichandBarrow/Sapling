# Learnings

Living document of non-obvious insights from Kay's search fund journey. Used by the Niche Intelligence skill to inform what makes a good niche and what pitfalls to avoid. Updated as new learnings emerge from calls, deals, and research.

Last updated: 2026-03-16

---

## Industry Structure

- **Art collection management software has a deceptively small market.** The ArtBinder founder said "the art space is very niche, she always felt like there was a ceiling." Galleries resist paying for software — collectors "will spend millions on a painting but save money on shipping and insurance."

- **Art storage is a balance sheet business, not an operating business.** Two investors independently flagged this. Capital intensity of climate-controlled facilities compresses margins. One said "they couldn't give me advice because they themselves don't manage a balance sheet business."

- **Art storage/logistics revenue is overwhelmingly project-based** (~60% transport, ~15% services, only ~15% storage). The recurring revenue that makes search funds attractive barely exists. Hangman's EBITDA margins were only ~5-8%.

- **In the art world, the gallery is the real customer, not the collector.** Client acquisition flows through referral chains: gallery staff gives clients 2 options with prices. Art advisors call their few trusted companies. "Clients use the companies based on who the gallery tells them to use."

- **Luxury watch repair is not standalone** — operates within retailers and independent retailer locations. Not a separate acquirable business unit. (From call with Kay's mother)

- **Constellation/Volaris quietly consolidated art collection management platforms** (ArtBinder, Gallery Systems). The ArtBinder founder said "they saw the writing on the wall." PE seeing the ceiling too is a signal.

- **Fine art insurance brokerage has a structural moat:** hard asset collections "CANNOT go under the household policy as it ends with the occupant's death." Big firms are "terrible" — "they only update appraisals by category" and "hire kids 2 years out." White space for specialized brokers.

- **Insurance brokerage acquisitions have strong PE exit potential.** Risk Strategies is actually a PE roll-up of smaller specialty firms. Clear exit path: build/buy specialty brokerage, grow it, sell to roll-up.

- **Women's health B2B looks promising macro but is "nascent" at company level** — most targets are VC-backed startups, not established businesses. Greenfield = startup land = wrong for search.

- **Estate planning has a hidden customer awareness problem:** "many people don't know they need this service till an issue arises." Demand exists but activation is unpredictable.

- **The largest art dealers face the greatest cost pressures** — highest-revenue clients are the most price-sensitive. ~30% of dealer expenses from external services across ~$20B market.

## Deal Evaluation

- **Seller wanting revenue multiples instead of EBITDA is a red flag.** Hangman owner "clearly stated he didn't want it valued off of EBITDA." When financials showed ~8% EBITDA, the preference made sense — masking poor profitability.

- **Unprepared sellers genuinely cannot produce adjusted EBITDA quickly.** "Creating an adjusted EBITDA is not something that can be done quickly... businesses getting ready to sell sometimes spend years preparing." Proprietary deals require patience.

- **Multi-partner ownership is a deal-killer for search fund investors.** Acumen (4 partners, different life stages, different equity) was interesting operationally but investors "don't want to take on a business with these previous equity arrangements."

- **Greenfield opportunity followed by revenue quality** are the two most important metrics for successful searches, per investor feedback.

- **"Growth cures everything" only works when asset-light.** An investor said this about growth, but another countered that capital intensity in art storage "makes it challenging from an investment perspective" even with growth.

- **Pre-LOI, you can get away with remarkably little information.** Investor recommended getting just enough to "put together an LOI quickly" rather than doing deep diligence before the offer.

- **Seller trust-building often precedes financial disclosure.** A seller requested references from Kay before sharing financials. Investor wanted a site visit before financials. Trust first, numbers second in proprietary deals.

- **Business model transformation deals can't use bank debt.** SonarWiz (one-time purchase → subscription) used "all investor equity and seller note" because "the change would engage any covenants on bank debt."

## Market Dynamics

- **Art market stays at $63-68B but exhibits "significant volatility without long-term value growth."** Three countries = 75%+ of market. Small TAM can be double-edged: manageable competition but limited total spend.

- **When art values decline, collectors store rather than sell** — temporarily benefiting storage. But "any slowing of money moving means the decline of an industry." Want industries where money is picking up pace, not parking.

- **PE market is in a buyer's market:** "no one's investing in funds. Companies to buy but can't get investors to put in more capital because they haven't seen returns." Less competition for acquisitions but harder exits.

- **$84T wealth transfer** creates structural demand for wealth transfer education, family governance, estate planning services — independent of market cycles.

- **Escrow software thesis hit a wall:** no smaller escrow providers exist to grow into fine art niche. Pain point exists but willingness to pay does not — recurring pattern in HNW services.

## Outreach & Sourcing

- **Cold email is increasingly ineffective.** 300 searchers/year flooding the market. Sellers hear from searchers 1-10 times. Emails caught in spam. High-touch, personalized approaches win.

- **Third-party sourcing undermines personal brand advantage.** Investor: "the vibe goes away a bit if a third party is doing outreach." The seller is motivated to work with Kay specifically — outsourcing removes that.

- **The best cold outreach was personalized and connection-based.** Hangman: personalized LinkedIn message referencing NYU connection + specific business knowledge. Owner responded immediately.

- **NDA-to-close math: need 100 NDAs/year (8/month, 2/week).** NDA→LOI: 5-10%. LOI submitted→signed: <50%. Need 3-4 signed LOIs to close 1 deal.

- **River guides are the silver bullet.** Estate planning conference (NAEPC): one contact provided intros to Chartwell's founder, Heritage NY, JSW, plus confirmed specialty insurance thesis. Worth the time to find them.

- **Service providers as river guides:** accountants, lawyers, industry service providers know which businesses are ripe for transition. Art advisor introduced companies. Insurance contact from BofA provided exact list of specialty brokers.

- **Lead gen lists were consistently off-target** because buy box was too broadly defined. Narrowing to specific sub-niches with explicit NAICS codes, example companies, and good/bad fit examples dramatically improved quality.

- **In-person visits accelerate trust-building dramatically.** Investor recommended "one week a month to visit/drive to businesses in a different city."

## Scoring & Framework

- **Scorecard evolved significantly through use.** Key additions: TAM (initially missing), revenue concentration, revenue predictability (recurring %), Porter's Forces. Original over-indexed on growth, under-indexed on defensibility.

- **Industry and company scorecards must be separate.** Industry = macro conditions (growth, fragmentation, catalyst). Company = firm-specific (retention, unit economics, team). Prevents wasting time on companies in bad industries.

- **5 essential diagnostic questions:** (1) Top 3 P&L expenses? (2) Client retention? (3) What happens if service stops? (4) What makes clients choose you? (5) Can you grow with existing customers alone?

- **Macro industry attractiveness ≠ niche-level opportunity.** Commercial cleaning passed initial screening but failed on fragmentation, differentiation, and market growth at niche level.

- **Revenue quality > revenue growth.** Key components: % recurring, customer concentration (<30% from top 5), LTV:CAC (>3:1), switching costs. Initial scorecard weighted growth too heavily at 40%.

- **Emerging pattern from investor feedback:** compliance-adjacent, HNW clients, recurring B2B, remote-operable, process-improvement oriented businesses consistently scored highest.

## Strategic

- **"Shovel seller" framework produces best candidates.** Don't buy a healthcare practice — buy the compliance software practices need. Don't manage collections — insure them. Position at chokepoints where money MUST flow due to regulation or risk.

- **Aspirational model: Allyant (document conversion for Wells Fargo ADA compliance).** Compliance-driven, B2B, recurring, essential, regulatory tailwinds. Every thesis benchmarked against this archetype.

- **"Buy a business on the rise, get on the wheel and steer."** Target growing businesses where owner wants to retire, not turnarounds.

- **Capital-intensive businesses are structurally incompatible with search fund model.** Need high cash-flow-to-equity returns. Real estate, fleet, climate-controlled facilities = wrong model.

- **Iron Mountain portfolio as reverse-engineering tool:** look at what PE is buying, then find sub-scale companies in those niches. Validates thesis and confirms exit pathway simultaneously.

- **Keeping a "passed" list with documented reasons is as valuable as the active pipeline.** Accumulation of "nos" progressively sharpens buy box.

- **Platform integration plays require technical leadership.** Investor cautioned that cobbling together disparate solutions "is hard work, particularly for a leader without a technical background."

## Network & Relationships

- **Luxury background (Chanel) unexpectedly powerful** for building industry networks. "Owners have responded positively to the benefit of my prior experience in understanding client service level expectations."

- **Don't share niche thesis with all investors.** Share only with co-leads and trusted few. "I don't want anyone with funds to look there before I reach out to sellers."

- **Investor updates should follow board package format** but not disclose industry details in writing. "Screen share on a call if they want more detail."

- **Strongest deal leads come from personal network**, not cold outreach. Hangman came from knowing someone in the NYC art network.

## Operational

- **Biggest time-waster: overresearching industries before engaging companies.** Investor: "I would like to be reviewing an actual company by now." Spend 1 week on research, then immediately begin outreach.

- **CRM selection matters.** HubSpot only recognized contacts by email (problem: many contacts are phone-only). SalesFlare's kanban pipeline and LinkedIn sidebar proved more practical. Now on Attio.

- **Email deliverability is a real constraint.** 5-10/day ramping to 20-25/day to stay under spam radar. Automated sequences risk "burning your email address."

- **Division of labor:** Lead gen = list building (40 cos/day) + outreach. Analyst = industry research + scorecards + decks. Principal = owner conversations + investor relations + strategy. Analyst should NOT handle owner call prep.

- **Always review NDAs for internal comments before sending.** Legal team left visible footnotes — unprofessional first impression with seller.
