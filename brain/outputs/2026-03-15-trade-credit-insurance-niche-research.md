# Trade Credit Insurance — Niche Research Brief

Research for G&B search fund go/no-go decision. This is distinct from customs bonds and cargo insurance.

---

## 1. What Is Trade Credit Insurance?

Trade credit insurance (TCI), also called accounts receivable insurance or credit insurance, protects businesses against the risk of non-payment by their customers. When a company sells goods or services on credit terms (net 30, net 60, etc.), TCI covers the receivable if the buyer fails to pay due to:

- **Insolvency** — buyer goes bankrupt or enters administration
- **Protracted default** — buyer simply doesn't pay within a defined period (typically 90-180 days past due)
- **Political risk** — government actions (currency inconvertibility, war, embargo) prevent payment on international trades

**How a policy works:**
- Policyholder submits their customer portfolio (list of buyers they extend credit to)
- Insurer assigns credit limits to each buyer based on financial analysis
- If a covered buyer doesn't pay, the insurer pays 80-95% of the receivable (there's always a deductible/retention)
- Policies are annual, renewable, and priced as a percentage of insured turnover (typically 0.1% to 0.5% of covered sales)

**Who buys it:**
- Manufacturers selling to wholesalers/retailers
- Distributors with large receivable books
- Exporters (especially to emerging markets)
- Any B2B company with concentrated customer risk
- Banks/lenders who want receivables collateral enhanced
- Industries with thin margins where one bad debt can wipe out a year's profit

**Key industries:** Food & beverage (largest segment), textiles/apparel, chemicals, metals, electronics, automotive parts, building materials.

## 2. Market Size

**Global market:**
- 2025: ~$13 billion in gross written premiums
- Projected 2033-2035: $31-38 billion (depending on source)
- CAGR: ~11% (2026-2035)
- Europe dominates with ~31% market share; Asia Pacific is fastest growing

**US market:**
- The US is significantly underpenetrated compared to Europe. In Europe, TCI is standard practice — roughly 10-15% of B2B trade is covered. In the US, penetration is estimated at only 2-5%.
- US premium volume is estimated at $2-3 billion annually, making it roughly 15-20% of the global market.
- The low penetration is both a challenge (less awareness, harder to sell) and an opportunity (massive room to grow).

**Why low US penetration matters for this analysis:** The product is less understood in the US. Brokers who specialize in it have a genuine expertise moat — generalist P&C brokers often don't know how to underwrite or place TCI. This creates the niche specialization dynamic favorable for acquisition.

## 4. Broker/Intermediary Landscape — Fragmentation Analysis

**This is the critical section for acquisition targeting.**

The TCI broker market has a distinctive structure:

### Large brokerage houses (TCI as a specialty practice)
- **Marsh** — dedicated Credit Specialties practice
- **Aon** — Credit Solutions team
- **Gallagher** — Credit & Political Risk practice
- **WTW** (Willis Towers Watson) — Trade Credit team
- **Lockton** — growing TCI practice
- **USI** — some TCI capability

These handle the large corporate accounts. Their TCI teams are typically 5-20 people within much larger organizations.

### Specialist independent brokers (the acquisition sweet spot)
This is where fragmentation exists. There are an estimated **20-50 independent firms in the US** that specialize in placing trade credit insurance. Many of these are:
- Founded by former Big 3 employees (ex-Euler Hermes, ex-Coface, ex-Atradius underwriters who went to the broker side)
- Small teams (3-15 people)
- Revenue range: $1M-$15M
- High client retention (85-95% renewal rates)
- Relationship-driven — clients stay because the broker understands their specific industry and buyer portfolio

**Notable specialist firms include:**
- **Meridian Finance Group** — one of the largest independent TCI brokers in the US
- **RFR Insurance** — specialty TCI and political risk
- **AU Group** — global TCI broker with US presence
- **ACTI (American Credit & Trade Insurance)** — independent specialist
- **Securitas Global Risk Solutions** — TCI and surety specialty
- **Dan Staggs & Associates** — small specialist
- **Trade Credit Solutions** — independent broker

**Fragmentation assessment: MODERATE-HIGH.** The market is not as fragmented as, say, benefits brokerage, but there are enough independent specialists in the $2-10M revenue range to make a roll-up conceivable. The key constraint is that there simply aren't hundreds of these firms — you might find 30-50 in total across the US. A roll-up would require acquiring 3-5 to reach meaningful scale.

## 5. Revenue Recurring?

**Yes — strongly recurring.** This is one of the most attractive features of TCI brokerage.

- Policies are annual and renew every year
- Renewal rates are very high: **85-95%** is typical
- Clients rarely switch brokers because:
  - The broker has deep knowledge of the client's customer portfolio
  - Switching means re-underwriting hundreds of buyer credit limits
  - The relationship involves ongoing credit limit management throughout the year (not just annual placement)
- Revenue is commission-based on premiums, so it grows with the client's sales volume
- Some brokers also earn fees for credit management services, monitoring, and consulting

**Revenue quality is comparable to other insurance brokerage lines** — arguably better because the switching costs are higher (the data/knowledge transfer is painful).

## 6. Broker Margins

**TCI broker economics:**

- **Commission rates:** Typically 10-20% of premium, with some variance by carrier and book size. Larger books may command higher commissions or contingent/profit-sharing arrangements.
- **Gross margins:** 60-80% (similar to other specialty insurance brokerage — low COGS, mostly people cost)
- **EBITDA margins:** 20-35% for well-run specialist firms. Smaller firms (<$3M revenue) tend to run leaner with higher margins because it's often a founder + small team with low overhead.
- **Revenue per employee:** $150K-$300K is typical for specialist TCI brokers

**The margin profile is attractive** and consistent with what PE firms look for in insurance distribution. The main cost is people (producers and account managers), and there's operating leverage as the book grows because servicing is partially scalable.

## 7. PE Roll-Up Activity

**This is where it gets interesting — and nuanced.**

Insurance brokerage broadly has been the #1 PE roll-up sector for the past decade. Firms like:
- **Acrisure** (backed by multiple PE firms)
- **AssuredPartners** (GTCR)
- **NFP** (Aon acquired)
- **Hub International** (Hellman & Friedman)
- **Patriot Growth** (GI Partners)

...have done hundreds of insurance brokerage acquisitions. However, **their focus has been overwhelmingly on P&C and employee benefits brokerage**, not specialty lines like TCI.

**TCI-specific PE activity:**
- There is **no major dedicated TCI broker roll-up** that I can identify. This is partly because the total addressable market for TCI brokers in the US is relatively small (maybe $200-400M in total broker revenue across all specialists).
- Some of the large platform roll-ups (Acrisure, Hub) have acquired agencies that happen to have TCI capabilities, but TCI has not been a targeted acquisition thesis.
- In Europe, there has been more consolidation — AU Group has done some acquisitions of TCI brokers across countries.
- **Tinubu Square** (French insurtech) has raised PE money for TCI technology, not brokerage.

**What this means for you:**
- Positive: Less competition for deals. You won't be bidding against well-funded PE platforms specifically hunting TCI brokers.
- Negative: There may be a reason PE hasn't targeted this — the niche might be too small for their return thresholds. A $200M total addressable broker market is below what most PE platforms need.
- Opportunity: A search fund acquiring a $3-5M revenue TCI broker could be compelling precisely because it's below PE radar but has great unit economics.

## 8. Growth Drivers

**Tailwinds:**

1. **Trade volatility and economic uncertainty** — Recessions, tariffs, supply chain disruptions, and geopolitical risk all drive TCI demand. COVID-19 was a massive catalyst — many companies that never considered TCI started buying it after seeing customers go bankrupt.

2. **US market underpenetration** — At 2-5% penetration vs. 10-15% in Europe, there's a long runway for growth through education and awareness.

3. **Bank requirements** — Lenders increasingly require TCI on receivables used as collateral for asset-based lending. This is a structural driver that creates pull demand.

4. **Supply chain lengthening** — As supply chains get more complex and international, companies have more counterparty risk to manage.

5. **SME adoption** — The Big 3 have launched simplified products for smaller businesses (AIG's A/R Secure covers $10-100M revenue companies). This expands the addressable market.

6. **Regulatory changes** — Basel III/IV capital requirements incentivize banks to use credit insurance as a risk mitigant, driving institutional demand.

**Headwinds:**

1. **Low awareness in US** — Most US businesses don't know TCI exists. This means longer sales cycles and more education required.

2. **Concentration among carriers** — The Big 3 have enormous market power. If they decide to go direct or squeeze broker commissions, it hurts intermediaries.

3. **Benign credit environment** — Low default rates (which have been the norm for much of the last decade outside of COVID) reduce urgency to buy TCI. "Why insure against something that doesn't happen?"

4. **Price sensitivity** — Some buyers view TCI as expensive relative to their historical loss experience.

## 9. Right-to-Win: Luxury/Fashion Angle

**This is actually a legitimate angle, with caveats.**

**Why it works:**
- Fashion and luxury brands are significant users of TCI. When a luxury brand sells wholesale to department stores (Nordstrom, Neiman Marcus, Saks) or multi-brand retailers, they carry substantial receivable risk. Neiman Marcus went bankrupt in 2020. Barneys went bankrupt in 2019. These events validated the need for TCI in the luxury vertical.
- The fashion industry has unique credit risk dynamics: seasonal ordering, consignment arrangements, return provisions, markdown allowances. A TCI broker who understands these nuances provides genuine value.
- **Textile/apparel is already one of the largest TCI industry verticals** — so you wouldn't be creating demand from scratch.
- Luxury brands often work with factors (receivables financing companies). There's an existing relationship between TCI and factoring that creates cross-sell opportunities.

**Why it's limited:**
- Luxury/fashion is only one vertical within TCI. To build a $5-10M revenue TCI brokerage, you'd need to serve multiple industries. The luxury angle gives you a beachhead, not a full business.
- Most TCI broker acquisitions would come with an existing book that spans many industries. Your luxury expertise would be a differentiator for new business development but wouldn't be the core of the existing business.
- The luxury angle is more of a "story for sellers" (why you'd be a good steward of their business) than a true operational right-to-win.

**Verdict on right-to-win:** Moderate. Better than a generic acquirer, but not as strong as, say, your right-to-win in estate management or UHNW services where the luxury connection is more direct.

## 10. AI Disruption Risk

**Assessment: LOW-MODERATE for brokers, MODERATE-HIGH for carriers.**

**How AI is being applied in TCI today:**
- **Carrier side:** The Big 3 are investing heavily in AI/ML for credit risk scoring, automated credit limit decisions, and portfolio monitoring. Allianz Trade and Coface both have significant data science teams. This is where AI has the most impact — replacing human underwriters for routine credit limit decisions.
- **Claims prediction:** AI models predicting which buyers are likely to default, enabling proactive limit adjustments.
- **Insurtechs:** Companies like **Tinubu Square**, **Nimbla** (acquired by Coface), and **Hokodo** are building digital-first TCI products, but mostly for SME segments.

**Why brokers are somewhat protected:**
- TCI brokerage is fundamentally a **relationship and advisory business**. The broker helps clients understand their coverage, negotiate with carriers, manage claims, and adjust credit limits throughout the year.
- The complexity of TCI policies (custom terms for each buyer, dynamic credit limits, policy endorsements) makes full automation harder than in commoditized lines like auto or home insurance.
- Clients value a broker who understands their specific industry and can advocate with carriers during economic downturns (when carriers try to cut limits — exactly when clients need coverage most).

**Where AI could threaten brokers:**
- If carriers build self-service portals that make it easy for companies to buy and manage TCI without a broker (already happening somewhat for SME products)
- If insurtechs like Hokodo or Nimbla succeed in making TCI a seamless, embedded product (buy-now-pay-later meets credit insurance)
- The information asymmetry that brokers exploit (knowing which carrier will give the best terms for a specific risk) could be reduced by AI-powered comparison tools

**Net assessment:** TCI brokers have 5-10 years before AI meaningfully disrupts the advisory relationship for mid-market and large accounts. SME segment is more vulnerable to digital disruption. A well-run TCI broker focused on mid-market and above should be relatively safe for the acquisition holding period.

## Summary Scorecard

| Criterion | Rating | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Market size | B | US broker TAM ~$200-400M. Enough for a single acquisition, tight for a multi-deal roll-up |
| Revenue quality | A | Annual renewal, 85-95% retention, grows with client sales |
| Margins | A- | 20-35% EBITDA, 60-80% gross |
| Fragmentation | B | 20-50 specialists exist, but that's a small pool |
| Growth | B+ | 11% global CAGR, US underpenetrated, but awareness is a headwind |
| PE competition | A | Not a targeted PE thesis = less bidding competition |
| Right-to-win | B- | Luxury/fashion angle is real but limited; you'd need broader industry knowledge |
| AI risk | B+ | Low-moderate for brokers, protected by relationship complexity |
| Acquirability | B | Firms exist in $2-10M range; founders are often approaching retirement |
| Industry dynamics | B+ | Counter-cyclical (demand rises in downturns), growing awareness |

**Overall: B+ niche. Interesting but not a slam dunk.**

**The bull case:** You find a $3-5M revenue TCI specialty broker run by a retiring founder, with a loyal book, 90%+ retention, and 25%+ EBITDA margins. You use your luxury/fashion network to develop the vertical organically. The US market grows as penetration catches up to Europe.

**The bear case:** The niche is too small (only 30-50 US specialists), the Big 3 carriers squeeze broker commissions, and you struggle to grow because US companies still don't understand or want TCI. The luxury angle isn't enough to differentiate.

**Recommendation:** Worth a deeper look (1-2 expert calls with TCI brokers/underwriters) before making a go/no-go decision. Specifically validate: (a) actual number of independent US TCI brokers in the $2-10M range, (b) whether commission rates are stable or under pressure, (c) whether any founders are known to be looking for exits.
