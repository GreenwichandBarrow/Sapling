## 2. Insurance Ecosystem Map

The insurance industry has a massive B2B infrastructure layer that most searchers never see because they focus on brokerage (the client-facing front end). Here is the full map with target pool estimates:

### HIGH Target Pool (50+ independently owned firms)

**Premium Audit Services**
- What: Field inspectors who verify payroll, sales, and exposures for workers' comp and GL policies. Carriers outsource this.
- Target pool: 50-100+ firms. Many small regional operators.
- Revenue: Recurring (carriers need audits annually on every commercial policy)
- Margins: 20-30% (service-based, low overhead)
- PE saturation: LOW-MEDIUM. EXL acquired Overland Solutions (2014), but many independents remain.
- Named companies: Insurance Audit Services (est. 1989), Premium Audit Consultants, Accelevant Premium Audit, Lowry & Associates, NEIS
- Key question: Are individual firms large enough ($2-5M+ revenue)?

**Loss Control / Risk Engineering Services**
- What: Field inspectors who assess property risk, recommend safety improvements. Carriers and TPAs outsource.
- Target pool: 50-100+ firms. Highly fragmented with regional players.
- Revenue: Mix of per-inspection fees (recurring through carrier contracts) and consulting
- Margins: 25-35%
- PE saturation: LOW-MEDIUM. Global Risk Consultants (GRC) acquired Aon's field services (Jan 2026), but many small firms.
- Named companies: Cox and Smith Risk Management, Loss Control Services (est. 1996), National Risk Services, Midwest Technical Inspections, SCI Inspection, HSB Professional Loss Control
- Key question: Is this too close to "labor management" (field inspector workforce)?

**Insurance BPO / Back-Office Operations**
- What: Outsourced policy administration, certificate issuance, data entry, premium processing for agencies and carriers
- Target pool: 40-80 firms (U.S.-based, not offshore)
- Revenue: Recurring (per-policy or retainer based)
- Margins: 20-35% (depends on onshore vs. offshore model)
- PE saturation: LOW for small/mid-size players
- Named companies: Covenir (onshore BPO), Insuserve, Insurance Backoffice Pro, TPA Engine
- Key question: Is U.S.-based BPO defensible against offshore competition?
- SOURCE: [[entities/jeremy-black]] specifically mentioned "insurance agency back-office models creating scalable revenue from multiple agents servicing territories" in 2026-02-02 call

**IMO/FMO (Insurance Marketing Organizations)**
- What: Distribution intermediaries that recruit, train, and support independent insurance agents. Earn override commissions on all business written through their network.
- Target pool: 100+ firms nationally (highly fragmented)
- Revenue: RECURRING (trail commissions on policies remain in force)
- Margins: 15-25% (thin on individual policies but high-volume)
- PE saturation: MEDIUM. Roll-ups exist but market is still fragmented.
- Named companies: Senior Market Sales, PSM Brokerage, SafeMoney Broadcasting (maintains directory of 100+ IMOs)
- Key question: Do individual IMOs have enough revenue to meet search fund criteria?
- SOURCE: [[entities/jeremy-black]] mentioned his mother's client ran "a marketing organization that assembled broker/agent groups for writing a variety of annuity and life insurance policies" (Gmail, 2026-02-04). This is the IMO/FMO play he referenced.

### MEDIUM Target Pool (20-50 independently owned firms)

**Trade Credit Insurance Brokerage**
- What: Specialty brokers who place trade credit insurance for businesses to protect against customer non-payment. Under-penetrated in U.S. market.
- Target pool: 30-60 firms (small broker community, ICBA network has 900+ globally)
- Revenue: RECURRING (annual policy renewals, growing market)
- Margins: 25-35% (specialty insurance margins)
- PE saturation: LOW (niche within insurance, not targeted by roll-ups yet)
- Named companies: Trade Acceptance Group, Texel (fka Meridian), MFE Insurance, plus FCIA/Great American broker directory
- Kay's right-to-win: Brother in marine logistics (trade-adjacent), fashion brands need TCI for wholesale receivables
- Key question: Are there enough firms with $2M+ in commission revenue?
- SOURCES: [[entities/jeremy-black]] email (2026-02-04), Jeremy Black call (2026-02-02), already on Top 5 list, ICBA network directory

**Surplus Lines Compliance & Tax Filing**
- What: Outsourced filing of surplus lines taxes, stamps, and regulatory reports across all 50 states for E&S brokers
- Target pool: 20-40 firms
- Revenue: RECURRING (every surplus lines policy requires state filings)
- Margins: 30-45% (compliance + software platform model)
- PE saturation: LOW. Vertafore acquired InsCipher, but many independents remain.
- Named companies: InsCipher (now Vertafore), 3H Compliance Group, ReSource Pro Compliance (fka ILSA), Veracity Insurance
- Key question: How many are pure-play vs. divisions of larger compliance firms?
- CONNECTION TO IPLC: Same "embedded compliance" thesis as IPLC but applied to surplus lines rather than producer licensing

**Insurance Producer License Compliance (Service Bureaus)**
- What: Outsourced management of producer license renewals, CE tracking, state filings, appointments
- Target pool: 15-25 firms (as already identified -- thin but actionable)
- Revenue: RECURRING (per-producer monthly fees)
- Margins: 35-50%
- PE saturation: MEDIUM-HIGH (NLC Group is a target but PE is active)
- Named companies: NLC Group ($10.6M rev), ACCEL Compliance, 3H Compliance Group, Perr & Knight (broader), ReSource Pro Compliance
- Already on Top 5 list as niche #3. Time-sensitive.
- SOURCES: Already well-researched. Richard Augustyn's "embedded services" insight led here.

**Estate Management Companies**
- What: Full-service management of UHNW residential estates (staffing, maintenance, vendor coordination, budgets)
- Target pool: 25-40 firms
- Revenue: Recurring (monthly retainer per estate)
- Margins: 20-30%
- PE saturation: LOW (too fragmented and relationship-driven for PE interest so far)
- Named companies: Crown Estate Services, Celebrity Estate Management, XM Property Management, Nines (tech-enabled, VC-backed -- competitor not target)
- Kay's right-to-win: STRONGEST -- Chanel/luxury background IS the credential
- Key question: Can individual firms reach $2M+ revenue? Many are small.
- Already on Top 5 list as niche #2.

### LOWER Target Pool or Higher Risk

**MGA (Managing General Agent) -- Specialty Lines**
- What: Underwriting authority granted by carriers for specialty niches (cyber, cannabis, short-term rental)
- Target pool: 100+ but highly variable in size and model
- Revenue: Recurring (commissions + fees on policies underwritten)
- Margins: 25-40% (higher than retail brokerage)
- PE saturation: HIGH. McKinsey specifically identifies MGAs as PE targets. Aggregator platforms are actively rolling up.
- Risk: [[entities/hunter-hartwell]] specifically advised AGAINST MGAs -- "MGAs and wholesalers are less accessible for search fund operators" (2026-01-12 call)
- KILLED for retail MGA -- but SPECIALTY niche MGAs focused on a single product line may still have independently owned targets

**Actuarial Consulting**
- What: Outsourced actuarial services for small-to-mid carriers and self-insured entities
- Target pool: 30-50 firms
- Revenue: Mix of project and retainer
- Margins: 40-60% (pure intellectual capital)
- PE saturation: MEDIUM
- Risk: KEY PERSON risk is extreme -- actuaries ARE the business
- Named companies: Perr & Knight, Risk International (now Artex)
- KILLED: Fails key person risk filter

**Insurance Staffing/Recruiting**
- What: Specialized recruiters placing insurance professionals
- Target pool: 30-50 firms
- Revenue: Mix of placement fees and contract staffing
- Margins: 15-25%
- PE saturation: MEDIUM
- Named companies: The Jacobson Group, Questpro, The Jonus Group, The Newman Group, WAHVE
- Risk: Staffing is cyclical and relationship-driven
- TABLED: Doesn't meet recurring revenue filter cleanly

**Customs Bonds & Cargo Insurance**
- What: Specialty brokers filing customs bonds (required for all importers) and placing cargo insurance
- Target pool: 30-50+ firms
- Revenue: RECURRING (bonds renew annually, cargo insurance is ongoing)
- Margins: 20-30%
- PE saturation: LOW-MEDIUM
- Named companies: Trade Risk Group/Trade Risk Guaranty (mentioned by [[entities/jeremy-black]]), LOGISTIQ, International Bond & Marine Brokerage
- Kay's right-to-win: Brother works in marine logistics
- TABLED on KILLED list as "Customs Bonds & Cargo Insurance" but Jeremy's recommendation was specifically about specialty brokers, not customs brokerage itself
- Key question: Was this killed prematurely? The vertical Jeremy described is NOT customs brokerage -- it's customs bond specialty brokers. Different animal.

## 7. Recommended Discussion Topics for Tomorrow

### Decision 1: Should insurance back-office/infrastructure replace insurance brokerage as the primary thesis track?

Every call file confirms that front-end insurance brokerage (retail, specialty, art) has prohibitive multiples (12-14x EBITDA), thin independently-owned target pools, and no-introduction culture. But the INFRASTRUCTURE that supports brokerages -- premium audit, loss control, compliance filing, policy administration -- trades at lower multiples, has more fragmented ownership, and is operationally simpler.

This is NOT abandoning insurance. It's going from "I want to own a brokerage" to "I want to own the things brokerages can't function without."

### Decision 2: Which 3 niches to sprint on?

Recommended combination for "3 niches x 10 companies":

**Niche 1: Trade Credit Insurance Brokerage** -- Already has conviction, Jeremy Black as river guide, under-penetrated U.S. market, fashion/textile vertical as right-to-win angle. Sprint on building target list using ICBA network + FCIA broker directory.

**Niche 2: Premium Audit + Loss Control Services** -- Largest target pool in the insurance infrastructure space. Carriers MUST outsource this. Recurring revenue through annual carrier contracts. Named firms exist. Low PE saturation. Sprint on verifying firm sizes and independence.

**Niche 3: Surplus Lines Compliance & Tax Filing** -- Same "embedded compliance" thesis as IPLC but with a larger addressable market (surplus lines market is booming due to hard market). Regulatory-driven, recurring, asset-light. Sprint on identifying independently owned service bureaus vs. tech platforms.

**Backup: Insurance Back-Office BPO (U.S.-onshore)** -- If surplus lines compliance has too few independent targets, pivot to the broader insurance BPO space. Jeremy Black specifically validated this model.

### Decision 3: What to do about the Top 5 list?

The current Top 5 has two niches with very thin target pools:
- IPLC: 4-6 targets, PE rolling up (time-sensitive)
- Art Insurance Brokerage: 3-5 targets, consolidated

These may need to be replaced or demoted to "opportunistic" while insurance infrastructure niches get promoted.

### Decision 4: Follow up on Jeremy Black's two leads THIS WEEK

Both leads from [[entities/jeremy-black]] (back-office operations model + IMO/FMO) were mentioned 6+ weeks ago and never pursued. A simple follow-up email or call could unlock the most actionable intelligence in this entire report.

### Decision 5: Call August Felker with a specific question

Instead of a general "what do you think" call, ask [[entities/august-felker]] the precise question: "Which insurance service verticals -- not brokerage, but the operational infrastructure -- are still independently owned, fragmented, and under PE's radar?"

### Decision 6: Activate the carve-out path

[[entities/hunter-hartwell]]'s carve-out suggestion (buy a book of business or department from a larger firm) could work for BOTH the insurance brokerage thesis AND the trade credit insurance thesis. PE-backed brokerages regularly shed non-core specialty divisions. This dramatically expands the acquirable target universe.
