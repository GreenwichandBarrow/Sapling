# Specialty Coffee Equipment Service — Niche One-Pager

Research for G&B search fund go/no-go decision.

**Niche definition:** Independent service companies that maintain commercial espresso machines, grinders, and ancillary specialty coffee equipment for high-end coffee shops, boutique hotels with espresso programs, and fine-dining restaurants with bar-quality espresso programs.

**NOT in scope:** Kitchen hoods, refrigeration, combi ovens, dishwashers — different specialty teams with different certifications. Earlier inbox file (4/18) bundled these as "F&B Equipment Service" — that bundling was wrong.

---

## 1. Market Size and Structure

US specialty coffee market: ~$80B retail revenue (2024), growing 8-12% annually, well above broader food service. Out of an estimated 35,000+ specialty/3rd-wave coffee shops in the US, the high-end / professionally-equipped subset is ~10,000-15,000 cafes plus boutique hotel F&B programs, fine-dining espresso bars, and members-only club espresso programs.

The service market is fragmented:
- **~100-200 pure-play specialty espresso service shops** in the US
- Most metros have 2-5 shops; tier-1 metros (NYC, SF, LA, Chicago, Seattle) have 5-15 each
- Typical shop: 1-5 techs, $1-5M revenue, owner-operated
- OEMs (La Marzocco USA, Slayer Service, Synesso) have direct service arms in major metros but cannot cover the long tail or multi-brand fleets
- Long tail of regional specialty cafes and hotels relies entirely on independents

## 2. Equipment in Scope

- **Commercial espresso machines:** La Marzocco, Slayer, Synesso, Modbar, Victoria Arduino, Mavam, Rocket, Faema, Astoria, Nuova Simonelli
- **Commercial grinders:** Mahlkönig, Mazzer, Anfim, Eureka, Compak, Ditting
- **Brewers / batch (sometimes):** Fetco, Curtis, Bunn, Marco
- **Coffee-specific water filtration (sometimes):** Everpure, OptiPure, BWT
- **New install / commissioning** of espresso programs (revenue stream)

## 3. Customer Set

- **3rd-wave / specialty independent cafes** (Joe Coffee, Sey, Devoción, Stumptown, regional indies)
- **Boutique hotels with espresso programs** (Aman, Soho House, Casa Cipriani, NeueHouse, 1 Hotels, Cheval Blanc, Auberge)
- **Fine-dining restaurants with bar-quality espresso** (Michelin-tier bars, top NYC/LA/SF restaurants)
- **Members-only clubs with espresso programs** (Zero Bond, Core Club, Aman Club)
- **Specialty cafe chains** (Blue Bottle, % Arabica, Verve, Sightglass)
- **High-end catering / events** (recurring service for fixed equipment, project for events)

Customer concentration risk: hotel chain and cafe chain accounts can grow large; need to balance with broader independent base.

## 4. Business Model and Revenue Mix

| Revenue line | Recurring? | Margin profile |
|---|---|---|
| **Preventive Maintenance contracts (quarterly visits)** | TRULY recurring (same client YoY, contracted) | High margin, sticky |
| **Water filter changes (monthly)** | TRULY recurring | Moderate, scheduled |
| **Parts pass-through** | Recurring per-call on retained accounts | High margin (markup) |
| **Emergency repair calls** | Episodic but on retained client base | Highest margin (urgency premium) |
| **New install / commissioning** | Project-based | Highest absolute dollar |
| **Equipment sales** (some shops) | Project-based | Distribution margin |

**Recurring profile:** ~60-70% of revenue is contract or scheduled (PM + water + parts + retained-account repair). 30-40% project (new install + commissioning). Very strong recurring profile by Kay's strict definition.

**Typical margins:** 8-12% blended net at owner-operator level. Best-run operators with strong contract base hit 12-15%. AI / route optimization / professional management can lift to 15-18%.

## 5. Why Messy in Great Segment (3-Step Filter)

**Step 1 (Segment good):** ✅ Strong
- Specialty coffee market growing 8-12% annually (well above food service overall)
- Boutique hotels expanding F&B programs (post-COVID guest experience focus)
- Specialty cafe density continues to grow in tier-1 + tier-2 metros
- Insurance + brand-protection drivers (broken espresso machine = lost shift = lost revenue + brand damage)
- Zero downtime tolerance = price-insensitive demand for emergency service

**Step 2 (Operationally messy):** ✅ Strong
- Regional 1-5 tech shops, founder-led
- Manual scheduling, paper invoices, no platform
- Founder = master tech (succession risk = acquisition opportunity)
- No cross-shop benchmarking, no shared inventory, no shared parts pricing
- Customer service quality varies wildly — some shops have no after-hours coverage

**Step 3 (AI-resistant + recurring):** ✅✅ STRONGEST in our niche set
- Tech goes on-site to fix the machine — physical work, AI cannot virtualize
- Espresso pull tasting + adjustment requires human craft
- Boiler hydraulics, group head rebuilds, grinder calibration — physical
- Truly recurring per Kay's strict definition (PM contracts + water filter scheduled + retained-account repair)

## 7. Growth Path (Both/And, Sequenced)

**Year 1: Anchor acquisition + AI/ops professionalization**
- Apply route optimization (cut tech drive time 15-25%)
- Predictive maintenance via sensor data + AI (upsell PM contracts based on usage data)
- Customer self-serve portal (filter ordering, service requests, billing)
- Centralized parts inventory + pricing optimization
- Margin lift from 8-12% to 15-18%

**Year 2-3: First 1-2 tuck-ins (adjacent metros)**
- Centralize back-office (CFO, IT, customer service)
- Cross-sell across acquired books (cafes that use espresso also use water filtration, sometimes brewers)
- Manufacturer relationship leverage (combined volume = better parts pricing from La Marzocco USA, Slayer parts, Mahlkönig parts)
- Knowledge transfer of best techs across regions for high-complexity service

**Year 4-5: Scale to 4-5 metros**
- Route density per metro
- Top-tier hotel chain accounts (Aman portfolio, Soho House network, 1 Hotels)
- Specialty cafe chain accounts (Blue Bottle service contract, % Arabica national)
- National platform multiple at exit

- Year 3: $9-12M revenue, AI-lifted to 14-15% margin = $1.3-1.8M EBITDA

## 8. Where It Gets Harder

- **Manufacturer disintermediation:** La Marzocco USA + Slayer Service expanding direct factory service in major metros (NYC, SF, Seattle, LA). Limits independent service-only play in those specific metros for those specific brands. Mitigation: target multi-brand independents serving the long tail of brands (Synesso, Modbar, Victoria Arduino, Faema, etc.) where factory service doesn't reach.

- **Talent constraint:** Master-level espresso techs are scarce. Growth ceiling is hiring. Apprenticeship programs + factory-training partnerships needed.

- **Customer concentration:** Over-indexing to one chain (e.g., all revenue from Soho House) creates key-account risk. Balance institutional accounts with broader independent cafe base.

- **Competitive dynamic:** Some tech-forward startups (Espresso Parts, Pull Caffe, others) are organizing the market with online parts + tools. Watch for category-leader emergence; G&B should aim to BE the category leader, not compete with one.

- **Geographic constraint:** Service is regional — cannot be virtualized. Roll-up requires presence in each target metro. Tuck-in pace is paced by available targets in chosen metros.
