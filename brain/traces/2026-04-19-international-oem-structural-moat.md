## Trigger

Session moved from rejecting Commercial Laundry Equipment Service (confirmed consolidated by EVI + Alliance direct channel) to evaluating Specialty Coffee Equipment Service. CIO initially applied the same disintermediation concern to coffee. Kay countered: "because if the manufacturers are international, I can imagine them wanting local servicers."

## Decision

**Adopted a named structural distinction for evaluating equipment-service and authorized-dealer niches:**

- **Low disintermediation risk**: OEM headquartered overseas (especially Italian / European family-owned industrial groups) cannot economically run direct service across US metros. Structural anchor is authorized local dealer.
- **High disintermediation risk**: OEM is US-based and scale-sized to go direct (e.g., Alliance Laundry Systems — Ripon WI, PE-backed, now public). These are the Alliance / commercial-laundry trap.

Specialty coffee equipment (Italian-dominated: La Marzocco, Nuova Simonelli / Victoria Arduino, Gruppo Cimbali / Slayer, Sanremo, Astoria, Rancilio) clears low-risk. Commercial laundry (Alliance-dominated, US-origin) fails.

## Alternatives Considered

- **Treat all equipment-service niches identically with high disintermediation risk.** Rejected — fails to distinguish genuinely durable dealer networks (Ferrari/Lamborghini authorized service, 50+ year pattern) from actively-consolidating ones (Alliance direct channel).
- **Treat OEM origin as noise; just check for current direct-channel activity.** Rejected — structural factors (geographic distance, labor arbitrage reversal, cultural operating DNA, volume economics) predict future behavior more reliably than current snapshots.

## Reasoning

Why international OEMs structurally cannot run direct in US metros:

1. **Response-time SLA.** Specialty cafes lose revenue by the hour when equipment goes down. A cross-Atlantic direct service operation cannot promise <24hr response. Only a local dealer network can.
2. **Labor arbitrage reversal.** US service-tech wages ($50-80K) are lower than Italian tech wages plus travel plus import overhead. Going direct is uneconomic.
3. **Cultural / operating DNA.** Italian industrial groups have run on dealer ecosystems for 70+ years. Dealer distribution is embedded in how they operate — changing requires organizational will they rarely demonstrate.
4. **Volume economics.** US specialty coffee is meaningful but not massive relative to these OEMs' global revenue. Too small to justify direct infrastructure (service centers, parts depots, W-2 techs) across multiple US metros.
5. **Local knowledge premium.** Water chemistry, local regs, barista/roaster community dynamics — non-replicable from Italy.

The closest structural analogue is European luxury auto service (Ferrari, Lamborghini, Maserati, Bentley authorized dealer-service networks — stable for 50+ years with near-zero direct-channel encroachment).

## Why This Trace Matters

This is a **reusable niche-evaluation framework**, not a one-off finding. Future agents scoring equipment-service or authorized-dealer niches should apply this lens:

1. Identify the dominant OEMs in the category.
2. Classify each by origin (international/European vs US-based) AND by scale (PE-backed consolidator vs family-owned).
3. High disintermediation risk profile: US-origin + PE-backed/public + scale-sized to go direct.
4. Low disintermediation risk profile: international + family-owned + reliant on dealer channels.
5. Mixed profile (e.g., specialty coffee has Italian OEMs + US-origin Synesso/Modbar): score the OEM mix, flag the US-origin exposure separately, underwrite it as hybrid risk.

Without this framework, future analyses would over-generalize from commercial-laundry to all equipment-service categories and miss high-moat niches (coffee, wine equipment, luxury auto).

## Key Insight

**"Equipment service" is not one category for disintermediation risk — OEM origin determines the structural trajectory.**

A 30-second reusable heuristic: where is the OEM HQ on a map, and who owns it?
- Italy / France / Germany / Switzerland + family-owned or small-group → authorized dealer stable
- Wisconsin / Ohio / PE-backed / public → authorized dealer at risk, likely 3-5yr existential compression
