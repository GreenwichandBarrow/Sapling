## Trigger

Mid-session while reshaping Sam's DealsX vertical book. Kay held 4 SaaS + 2 services categories with Sam. CIO was pushing to trim SaaS to the single thesis-aligned category (Vertical SaaS for Luxury & High-Value Asset Service Industries). Kay articulated the underlying reason rather than accepting the mechanical trim: "perhaps the saas is an add on or gives us the behind the scenes software for managing the business or luxury services portfolio, etc... its not the main acquisition but part..."

## Decision

**Path A chosen: services businesses are G&B's primary acquisitions; SaaS becomes supporting capability.**

Applied as a portfolio reshape:
- Trim Sam's 4 SaaS categories to 1 (keep Vertical SaaS for Luxury & High-Value Asset Service Industries)
- Retire: Enterprise Software & Data Platforms B2B, Specialty Healthcare Software B2B, Female-Led Vertical SaaS B2B
- Wind down the 10 SaaS sub-niches on WEEKLY REVIEW to Active - Long Term (finish in-flight outreach, no new targets)
- Keep the tracker record per Kay: "we keep the record on our tracker" even as Sam's execution scope narrows
- Add 3 new services categories: Specialty Commercial Equipment Services, Specialty Storage & Handling for High-Value Collections, Specialty Pest & Environmental Management Services

## Alternatives Considered

- **Path B: Pause all SaaS outreach, come back opportunistically.** Sam's full bandwidth to services. Rejected because it eliminates optionality if a good SaaS tuck-in candidate appears mid-sprint.
- **Path C: Keep one SaaS on slower cadence (quarterly, not weekly).** Preserves optionality with less dilution. Rejected in favor of Path A's simpler single-active-category rule.
- **Keep all 4 SaaS categories.** Rejected because the thesis drift (SaaS-as-primary) was miscalibrated to how Kay actually wants to build G&B.

## Reasoning

The G&B Charter already positions the company as an operator of luxury-adjacent services businesses in the Alain Wertheimer / Jewel-stage archetype. SaaS-as-primary would fight that identity. The right frame is: services is the asset class; SaaS is the capability layer that professionalizes services operators or manages a broader portfolio. This also resolves the margin friction earlier in the session (Specialty Coffee Equipment Service at 8-12% baseline EBITDA is an awkward fit for a "good SaaS" portfolio but a reasonable fit for a services-primary operator who underwrites post-acquisition ops lift to 15-18%).

The kept SaaS category — Vertical SaaS for Luxury & High-Value Asset Service Industries — is the one that serves the services portfolio: private club mgmt software, jewelry retail ERP, marina/yacht mgmt software, luxury hospitality ops software, art storage & collections mgmt software. These are the "behind the scenes" tools Kay described.

## Why This Trace Matters

Future agents evaluating new niches must understand: **services with luxury/high-value customer bases are the primary-acquisition target; SaaS is evaluated as supporting capability, not as a standalone thesis.** This explains why a niche may score well mechanically (Healthcare Reg SaaS, 2.70) but still be wound down on the tracker — it doesn't fit the primary-acquisition identity.

Without this trace, a future agent would likely treat SaaS and services niches as equal candidates when scoring and would recommend re-activating the retired SaaS categories when they score well in subsequent runs. That would be wrong.

## Key Insight

G&B's thesis filter has two layers, not one:
1. **Does the niche fit the structural criteria?** (3-step filter, scorecard ≥2.5)
2. **Does the niche fit the identity?** (services with luxury/high-value customers = primary; SaaS = supporting capability only if it serves the portfolio)

A niche must clear both filters to be primary-acquisition material. Layer 2 was implicit before this session; now it's explicit.
