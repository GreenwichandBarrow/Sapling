## Trigger

Kay closed a major sprint-planning session with enthusiasm: "we have our 5 niches to focus on. Lets double down on these and get an LOI in 11 days."

Two viable responses:
1. Match energy, say "on it," build a 11-day sprint plan.
2. Reality-check the timeline against structural pipeline math and offer realistic alternate goals.

## Decision

**Chose #2.** Surfaced that LOI in 11 days from 5 new niches is structurally unachievable (cold-target-to-LOI typically 3-6 months; even warm pipeline usually 4-8 weeks). Asked which of the 5 niches Kay thought had a pre-existing warm pipeline close enough to LOI. Offered realistic 11-day sprint alternatives: first CIM in hand, first Thumbs Up call scheduled, two Thumbs Up calls + one NDA signed.

Kay accepted the reality-check, pivoted to building warm-intro activation across the 5 niches instead of forcing the 11-day LOI frame.

## Alternatives Considered

- **Match energy, build the 11-day plan anyway.** Rejected — would set a false expectation, produce disappointment when the math doesn't land, and erode trust in future CIO judgments.
- **Decline silently without offering alternatives.** Rejected — leaves Kay with no actionable target. The CIO role is to render verdicts AND offer the path that pencils.
- **Ask Kay which deal she has in mind first, then commit.** Closer to what was done. But without explicitly framing why 11 days is hard, Kay might have named a niche and I'd still have ended up over-promising. Framing the structural timeline first was the right order.

## Reasoning

The CIO role explicitly renders verdicts, does not execute. When a CEO sets an aspirational goal that the pipeline math doesn't support, the CIO's job is to reality-check before execution — not after, when commitments have been made.

Core G&B principle applied: `feedback_search_fund_action_mandate` says "Wait vs act is never a real binary" — so the question is not whether to act but WHAT, WHEN, HOW. Applied here: not "should we pursue LOI" (yes, always) but "what's the highest-leverage 11-day milestone that IS achievable."

The reality-check itself converted aspiration → specific operational asks: Kay pivoted from "get LOI" to a rich warm-intro activation across all 5 niches (Sarah de Blasio, Mark, Amanda, Levi, Paul, Karaugh, Jason, Marriott luxury partnership director). That's a direct win from honest recalibration — faster pipeline progress in the real sprint than the false sprint.

## Why This Trace Matters

**Future agents face this pattern frequently.** A high-energy CEO sets an aggressive timeline or numerical goal. The agent's default failure mode is to match the energy and commit to execution, then under-deliver. The right pattern is:

1. Run the structural math silently (or in thinking).
2. If the math doesn't support the goal, surface the gap explicitly with specific numbers.
3. Offer 2-3 realistic alternate goals at specific achievability tiers.
4. Ask for CEO's pick OR accept CEO's counter (she may know something the math missed).

This is NOT hedging or deflecting. It's the CIO function — render verdicts, don't execute on premises that don't pencil.

**Specifically for deal-to-LOI timelines**, durable rule: cold-target-to-LOI is 3-6 months; warm pipeline is 4-8 weeks; existing active-diligence deal is 2-4 weeks. Any goal tighter than those tiers needs an explicit named warm deal to justify the compression.

## Key Insight

**Aspirational timelines test the CIO's willingness to push back, not test execution capacity.** The right response is honest recalibration that converts aspiration into an achievable specific milestone. Kay's actual intent wasn't "literally 11 days to LOI" — it was "accelerate to first deal." Reality-checking revealed that, and Kay's warm-intro sprint is a better fit for that underlying intent.
