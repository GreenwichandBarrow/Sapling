# Decision Trace: OEM Authorized-Dealer Structure is a Positive Signal

## Context

Guillermo (Ashford Ventures) flagged OEM factory-service structure as a skepticism vector during the 2026-04-21 biweekly — his framing: "factory service" may be branded third parties operating exclusively, which could shrink the independent-servicer acquisition universe. Claude scoped a research sprint to investigate W-2 vs. contracted servicer structure across 8 premium espresso OEMs. Kay called the question mid-research.

## Decisions

### Interpretation of OEM authorized-dealer model
**AI proposed:** Treat OEM authorization structure as an open-question DD item requiring primary-call validation to determine whether the model helps or hurts the acquisition thesis.
**Kay's response:** "OEM further supports our thesis. Skip that for now."
**Chosen:** OEM-authorized independent-dealer network is a POSITIVE signal for G&B's acquisition thesis, not a risk. The authorized-dealer ecosystem is exactly the target pool G&B wants to acquire in — dealers carry OEM authorization as a moat, build deep customer relationships, and are the fragmented operators the thesis targets.
**Reasoning:** The hostile scenario for G&B is OEMs bringing service in-house (W-2), which collapses the acquisition universe. Authorized-dealer networks preserve the independent pool while adding a moat (OEM certification as barrier to entry). Thesis-positive.
**Pattern:** #pattern/moat-signal-reframe

## Learnings

- When scoring equipment-servicing niches on the G&B industry scorecard, treat OEM-authorized independent-dealer networks as POSITIVE on defensibility (moat) and fragmentation (acquisition universe size).
- Treat "OEM brings service in-house / W-2" as NEGATIVE — it collapses the acquisition universe.
- Don't re-open the OEM-structure question in future DD sprints unless a specific OEM has publicly announced service insourcing.
