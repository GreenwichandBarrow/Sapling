# Decision Trace: Lead the draft with what THEY cared about, not what I noticed

## Context

Drafting Kay's outreach to William Hoffman (President, Hoffman Exterminating Co.) after Kay attended his NJPMA workshop presentation 2026-04-29.

My v1 draft opened with industry consolidation data Hoffman cited — Rentokil $3.4B, Rollins $3.3B, Annie CMEX, Ned's $140M+. That's the kind of opener I'd appreciate from a researcher who'd done their homework.

Kay corrected: *"hoffman talked multiple times on how he liked the community aspect of the work. how these businesses are really connected to the community. he sees it as mostly customer service with a little extermination thrown in."*

Then later: *"Ive shared the information about his commitment and passion for the community connect in the hopes that you would update the drafted, knowing this."*

Translation: she shared the community-fit insight specifically so I'd lead the draft with it. My consolidation-data opener showed I'd captured the data points but missed what the human actually cared about.

## Decision

Reopened the Hoffman draft with his community-fit insight as the first substantive sentence:

> "I attended your presentation at the NJPMA workshop today at Topgolf Edison and was struck by your framing of pest management as fundamentally a community-connected business. The line that stayed with me is that the work is mostly customer service with a little extermination thrown in. That reframed how I'm thinking about the industry, and it's already changing how I'd evaluate any operator I might meet."

Then, only after that, the G&B introduction + the "buy-and-operate, not roll-up" framing that directly mirrors what Hoffman cared about preserving.

## Why this matters for future agents

When drafting outreach to someone Kay has heard speak (workshop, podcast, conference panel, Zoom session, etc.), the opener should reference what the SPEAKER was passionate about, not what was most data-rich for our purposes.

**Test for any opener:** if the recipient read the first sentence and thought *"oh, this person actually listened,"* it works. If they thought *"this person caught the headline numbers,"* it doesn't.

This isn't about flattery. It's about signaling that Kay engaged with the content the speaker actually cared about — which is what builds the relationship rooted in their values, not just our deal needs.

## How to spot what they cared about

Cues from a Granola transcript or Kay's notes:
- **Repetition** ("hoffman talked multiple times on how he liked the community aspect")
- **Tone shifts** (when the speaker drops industry stats and talks about *meaning*)
- **Personal anecdotes** vs market data
- **What they pushed back on** — what they argued for or against reveals values
- **Kay's own emphasis** — if Kay surfaces a particular insight to me, that's a signal

Industry stats / multiples / scale numbers are table stakes (the speaker knows we know). The values-laden statements are where to lead.

## Litmus for future agents

For any cold-outreach draft to a person Kay has heard speak, ask:
1. What did this person say that wasn't a data point?
2. What did they emphasize through repetition or tone?
3. What did Kay specifically surface from the talk?

Lead with that. Move data to the second paragraph or skip entirely.
