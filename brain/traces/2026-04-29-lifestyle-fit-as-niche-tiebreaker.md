# Decision Trace: Lifestyle / dress-code fit as a niche-tiebreaker, not a primary criterion

## Context

Kay attended NJPMA Business Management Workshop 2026-04-29 at Topgolf Edison NJ. After the event, she reflected:

> "I liked the casualness of the pest management meeting today. I feel like I can dress preppy and casual as a business leader, not have to be all dressed up as I have been for over a decade. I got very tired of the office impression needed and I think it might continue if I go into art insurance."

This is a lived-experience signal — after 10+ years in luxury / corporate / financial-services environments requiring formal dress, casual operator dress codes (NJPMA pest workshop vibe) felt like relief.

## Decision

Save as `project_kay_lifestyle_dress_filter.md` with explicit framing:

- **NOT a primary kill criterion.** Niches with positive strategic merit (e.g., art insurance per Margot's specialty insurance brokerage thesis) don't get auto-dropped because of dress code.
- **Tiebreaker between similarly-scored niches.** When two niches rank close on traditional searcher criteria (TAM, recurring revenue, fragmentation, growth, margins), lifestyle-dress-fit becomes a deciding factor.
- **Don't surface in scorecards.** Per `feedback_niche_ranking_searcher_criteria_only.md`, niche rankings under acquisition-timeline constraints use traditional searcher criteria on external data — NOT internal-network depth or personal-fit factors. Lifestyle stays out of formal scoring.
- **DO use in 1:1 strategic conversations.** When Kay is weighing finalists or asking "which feels right," lifestyle-fit is fair game.

## Why this matters for future agents

- **Lived experience matters.** Per `feedback_kays_approach.md`, Kay values lived experience. After a decade of luxury-services dress codes, the cost of perpetuating that is real.
- **Positive niches by lifestyle fit:** specialty pest management, specialty equipment service, blue-collar services, lower-mid-market manufacturing, distribution, heritage holdings.
- **Negative-fit niches (don't drop, but weight against):** art insurance, private banking, wealth advisory, luxury services to UHNW clients, art storage if Kay personally engages collectors.
- **Caveat:** Kay can hire customer-facing executives in any niche; she doesn't have to be the front-facing dressed-up face. Lifestyle filter mostly applies to her own day-to-day operator role.

## Litmus for future agents

When two niches score similarly on traditional searcher criteria and Kay asks "which one," ask: which lets Kay show up as her preferred self (preppy-casual operator vs. formal corporate executive)? That's the tiebreaker.

Don't preemptively drop strategically valid niches because of this filter. Surface it only when relevant to a finalist comparison.
