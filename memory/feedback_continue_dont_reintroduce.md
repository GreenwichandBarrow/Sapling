When Kay's follow-up email is to someone she had a substantive in-person conversation with, **the email picks up where the conversation left off** — it does not re-introduce, re-pitch, or re-state things already covered in person.

**Why:** Pattern reinforced 2026-04-25 across two drafts.
1. Andrew Lowis (XPX): Kay cut "you handled the room well" because she'd already said it in person. Stating it again in writing diminishes the original moment.
2. Matt Luczyk (XPX): Kay said "I already told him the kind of businesses I look at" — when v2 draft re-pitched the buy-box criteria as if introducing them, it broke the conversational continuity. The email should *reference* what was already said, not *re-state* it.

The two cases share a principle: **what's been said in person is already received**. Re-stating in writing reads as either pitch-mode (she's selling herself again) or as if she forgot the in-person exchange happened. Both undermine the relationship.

**How to apply:**

- **Compliments:** said in person → don't repeat in writing. (Andrew Lowis precedent.)
- **Buy-box / what Kay's looking for:** said in person → reference it as a callback, not a re-pitch. ("Anything else from your radar that fits what we discussed" not "we focus on asset-light operating businesses in LMM, particularly...")
- **Background on G&B:** said in person → don't re-describe. The email assumes the relationship has memory.
- **Deal-specific context Matt or another intermediary shared:** can be referenced lightly ("the client situation you mentioned") without re-narrating the details they gave verbally — that protects sensitive info from being on email and respects their gatekeeper role.
- **What CAN be in the email:** scheduling logistics, callbacks to prior content as references, new context that wasn't covered in person, or specific next-step asks.

**Litmus test before sending:** read the draft and ask "would the recipient think Kay forgot we had this conversation?" If yes, cut the re-introductory language.

**Distinct from cold outreach:** for cold outreach to strangers, full self-introduction is needed. This rule applies specifically to *follow-ups* after substantive in-person conversation.
