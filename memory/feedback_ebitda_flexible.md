Declared Apr 14 2026, during vertical SaaS Deal 1 criteria refinement.

**The rule:** Don't apply a hard EBITDA floor (e.g., $2.5M) when filtering deals or evaluating first-pass targets. Reported EBITDA is often understated; adjusted EBITDA typically reveals 20-50% more through owner add-backs, one-time expense normalization, and comp adjustments.

**Why:**
1. Kay has seen businesses double their EBITDA during deal negotiations through legitimate adjustments
2. Her investor explicitly advised "be open to smaller" — imposing a hard EBITDA floor closes off deals the investor would want to see
3. Adam Coffey's ECP pattern started small (below what a $2.5M EBITDA floor would have allowed) and grew into the economics

**How to apply:**
- When filtering niches or targets, prefer "profitable" or "positive EBITDA" over a specific dollar floor
- Sub-$2.5M EBITDA deals stay in the evaluation pool — don't auto-reject
- Run full diligence to determine adjusted EBITDA before rejecting on size
- Acknowledge that sub-$2M EBITDA Year 1 may mean leaner CEO comp initially, offset by growth-to-exit carry upside
- For deal shape on Sam / other sourcing channels: state "profitable, open to smaller sizes" rather than a hard number
