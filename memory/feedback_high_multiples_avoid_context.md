When public market data, panel reports, or M&A intel surface a sector running at "double-digit multiples" or being called a "seller's market" or "high competitive bidding" environment — Kay's read (2026-04-23 XPX panel discussion):

**This is context awareness, NOT a scoring change.** Do not codify it as a +/- in the niche scorecard. Do not add a "buyer-competitiveness" weighted line item. Just note it for situational read.

**Why:** A high-multiple environment means buyers are paying premium prices in that sector. As an acquirer, that's a NEGATIVE for our terms — we can't win on price against bidders willing to stretch. But the underlying business quality may still be strong, which is what the scorecard measures. Mixing market-pricing context into the business-quality score muddies both signals.

**How to apply:**
- When summarizing M&A intel or panel takeaways, *mention* if a sector is running hot ("MSPs / Atlassian-ServiceNow trading at high multiples per XPX 4/23 panel") — Kay reads it as a flag.
- Do NOT then propose "downgrade" or "deprioritize" the related niche on the scorecard. The scoring stays.
- If Kay explicitly decides to deprioritize based on the multiple data, that's a separate Kay decision recorded in session-decisions, not a scoring rule.
- Examples she flagged off-thesis from XPX 4/23 (high-multiple sellers' markets): Transportation +20% YoY, Business Services (MSPs / Atlassian / ServiceNow), Healthcare supply-constrained subsegments.

**Companion observation (also context-only, not scoring):** Funds are crowding $1–3M EBITDA mandates (95% of committed-capital funds per XPX). Above $3M EBITDA, fund competition thins. G&B's buy-box stays $1.5–5M EBITDA — no narrowing — but the $3–5M slice is the open lane within our band. Useful for prioritization within an otherwise-tied target pool, not a buy-box edit.
