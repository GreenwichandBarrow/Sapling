# G&B Investors Are Industry-Agnostic (Apr 28 2026)

**Rule:** G&B's 12 LPs are largely industry-agnostic. They evolve with macro / AI trends and generally avoid retail. Otherwise they believe in **classic search-friendly business structures** (recurring revenue, fragmented markets, defensibility, clean entity). They do NOT have specific industry preferences per LP. Don't ask "what's Jeff Stevens's industry preference?" or "what does Guillermo specialize in?" — that premise is wrong.

**Why:** Kay confirmed Apr 28 2026 during buyer-fingerprint interview. She rejected the premise of mapping each LP to specific industries directly: *"largely the investors are industry agnostic. They evolve of course with the larger macro / AI trends and generally stay away from retail but other than that they believe in the classic search friendly business structures but dont have specifically any industry mapping."*

This means investor pattern-matching observations (e.g., "Jeff's Anacapa portfolio is vertical SaaS heavy") describe what their portfolios HAPPEN to contain, not what they REQUIRE from new deals.

**How to apply:**

When evaluating niche fit for the investor-narrative dimension, score on:
- **Hard exclusions** (existing memory rules): no retail (Kay-stated Apr 28), no California, no lending, no carve-outs
- **Classic search-friendly criteria**: recurring revenue, fragmented market, defensible position, clean entity, asset-light, services / SaaS / infrastructure-leaning
- **Macro tailwind alignment**: AI-friendly, current-cycle aligned

When evaluating network leverage for a niche, **the investor leg of the network does NOT contribute niche-specific advantage.** Score network leverage on:
- Family network (mom — jewelry; husband — fine dining/hospitality; brother — marine logistics)
- Advisor network (Colin, Sam)
- Co-located firms (BK Growth, Saltoun, Tom Jackson, Clayton Sachs) = casual cross-pollination only, not niche-specific

If a future agent asks me "what's investor X's industry preference?" — answer: they don't have one beyond search-friendly + macro alignment + retail-avoidance.

**Posture (added Apr 28 2026 same session):** investors present as **conservative** and **poke holes in opportunities**, not endorse them. In a traditional search fund, the burden is on Kay to fight for the business — pitch (a) why it's a great deal, (b) why she's the right one to run it. Niches must survive hostile probing on standard objections: "why this niche over X?", "what if the macro turns?", "why are you the right operator?", "what's the moat?", "what's the multiple-expansion thesis?"

Implication for niche scoring: niches with **easily-defensible fundamentals** (clean recurring rev, identifiable moat, obvious tailwind, clear searcher fit) outperform niches requiring **complex narratives** even when paper-scoring is higher. Investors won't do narrative-construction work for Kay. If she can't answer the standard objections in one sentence each, the niche won't pass.

## Current macro state (as of 2026-04-28)

The "macro/AI trends" framing is not theoretical. As of late April 2026, an investor told Kay that he and other investors would be **hard-pressed to commit to any SaaS investment** given current macro conditions. **SaaS is OFF for now** as a viable category for G&B Deal 1.

This is a current macro filter, not a permanent rule — it sits alongside the other anti-niches (no retail, no California, no lending, no carve-outs) but is the only one that's macro-cyclical. The Apr 14 Charter declaration of Vertical SaaS as Bridge category was retracted Apr 28 2026 as a result.

**How to apply:** When evaluating any SaaS-LABELED niche right now (vertical SaaS, infrastructure SaaS, healthcare SaaS, etc.), surface the macro-state warning. Don't rank a SaaS-labeled niche as Bridge-viable until investor sentiment on SaaS shifts.

**Critical distinction (Kay Apr 28 2026):** the LABEL "SaaS" is currently out, but the ATTRIBUTES that made SaaS attractive remain in scope. Kay specifically named the attributes she still wants: **asset-light, highly scalable, remote-manageable, AI-tailwind upside.** The Bridge hunt is for businesses with this attribute-profile that are NOT labeled / valued / structured as SaaS deals — tech-enabled services with software-like contracts, specialty data/information businesses with subscription revenue, compliance/regulatory tech running as services, AI-native tools without SaaS-multiple baggage, vertical platforms that look more like services than software. Don't reject a niche just because it shares attributes with SaaS; reject it only if it's classified, sold, or valued as a SaaS deal.
