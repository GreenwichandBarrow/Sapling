When evaluating premium equipment servicing niches (specialty coffee equipment, commercial kitchen, medical equipment, elevator, etc.), the fact that OEMs run an "authorized dealer" or "authorized service provider" network of independent companies — rather than W-2 factory-employed technicians — SUPPORTS G&B's acquisition thesis. It does not undermine it.

**Why:** Kay's 2026-04-21 call with Guillermo Lavergne (Ashford) surfaced the OEM-service-structure question as a skepticism vector — Guillermo's framing was that "factory service" is often branded third parties operating exclusively, which could shrink the independent-servicer acquisition universe. Kay's judgment on reflection: the authorized-dealer ecosystem is exactly the target pool G&B wants to acquire in. These dealers carry OEM authorization as a moat (barrier to entry), build deep customer relationships, and are the fragmented operators G&B's thesis targets. An OEM that brings service in-house (W-2) is actually the hostile scenario — it shrinks the acquisition universe. The authorized-dealer model is the friendly one.

**How to apply:**
1. When scoring equipment-servicing niches on the G&B industry scorecard, treat OEM-authorized independent-dealer network as a POSITIVE on defensibility (moat) and fragmentation (acquisition universe size).
2. Treat "OEM brings service in-house / W-2" as a NEGATIVE — it collapses the acquisition universe and creates direct OEM competition.
3. Do not re-open the "OEM structure question" in future DD sprints unless the specific OEM in question has publicly announced a service-insourcing move. Default: authorized-dealer = thesis-positive.
4. Applies broadly across premium equipment servicing (medical, elevator, commercial kitchen, refrigeration, HVAC) — same logic.
