PE-vibe in owner-facing copy is structural, not lexical. Sentences like *"we hold for the long run / we don't run auctions / we look for businesses that... / built for owners not for exits"* read as PE marketing regardless of how warm the surrounding words are. The "we / we don't / for / not for" pattern is the tell, and readers code the writer as PE on rhythm before reading the meaning.

**Why:** On the 2026-05-04 website Day 1 mockup I built a 4-pillar "What an owner deserves" section ("we hold for the long run / we don't run auctions / we respect the transition / we protect what already works"). Kay's reaction: *"It sounds like private equity."* This was AFTER she had already chosen full Shoreham model with no founder on the site. The we-claims read PE despite no founder visibility.

Earlier in the same session I had pressure-tested her assumption that anti-PE feel comes from hiding the founder, suggesting it actually comes from copy. The Day 1 result confirmed the diagnosis.

**How to apply:** When writing any G&B-facing artifact (website, broker outreach, owner outreach, one-pager, deck), avoid:
- "We [verb] X" claim patterns describing what G&B does
- "We don't [verb] X" claim patterns describing what G&B avoids
- "Built for X, not for Y" oppositional framing
- "What we look for" / "what we value" headers that lead a list of firm criteria
- "Our approach is to..." / "Our philosophy is..." openings

Replace with observations:
- *"Some businesses run on the kind of trust that takes decades to build and only one bad transition to lose."*
- *"The work looks operational on the surface. Underneath, the real business is the relationship."*
- *"The customer cannot choose to go without; they can only choose who provides it."*

The site (or email, or one-pager) describes the WORLD G&B serves — the kind of business, the kind of owner, the kind of transition. The reader recognizes themselves. The implication that G&B is a fit-buyer arises from the reader's recognition, not from G&B asserting it.

Necessary "we" usage — firm identification at bottom of page, contact CTA, sender attribution — is fine. The doctrine is about CLAIMS, not pronouns.
