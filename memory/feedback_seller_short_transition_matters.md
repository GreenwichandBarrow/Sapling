A consistent seller pain point for founder-led / family-owned businesses at the G&B size band is **long post-close transition obligations**. Sellers don't want to be forced into 2-3 year consulting agreements, long earn-outs, or extended hand-holding. They want a clean exit option — with the ability to stay if both sides want it, not as a contractual obligation.

**Why:** Surfaced at XPX conference 2026-04-23 as a theme from intermediary conversations. Matches what Kay has been hearing from advisors and aligns with the G&B "legacy seller wants to pick their buyer" thesis. A founder who has run their business for 20-30 years is thinking about retirement, grandchildren, freedom — not another three years locked into a deal structure.

**How to apply:**

**In outreach (buy-box paragraph to intermediaries):**
- Use the updated buy-box language: "aim to retain existing employees, and are able to partner with a seller on terms in a customized way."
- "Customized terms" is the code for: short transitions, flexible earn-outs, whatever the seller needs.
- Do NOT explicitly say "we want short transitions" or "we don't require multi-year lock-ups" — that draws attention to the obligation they're worried about. Just imply flexibility via "customized."

**In first owner calls:**
- Ask the seller's transition preference early: "What does your involvement look like post-close? 3 months? A year? We're flexible."
- If they want short, that's GOOD — signal alignment.
- If they want long, fine — structure accordingly.
- Never pitch a default multi-year consulting requirement. Start from zero and negotiate up only if the seller asks.

**In LOI / deal structure:**
- Default to short transition (3-6 months) in initial LOI unless seller indicates otherwise.
- Earn-outs tied to long tenure are a red flag for G&B-fit sellers — they want freedom.
- Consider seller notes or rollover equity as alternatives to long consulting arrangements.

**In diligence:**
- Assess how critical the owner is to daily ops. If the business is too owner-dependent to survive a short transition, that's a diligence issue to flag (may affect valuation or require a strong GM in place).
- Retained employees + strong GM + documented processes = short transition is feasible.

**Signal check:** If a seller or their advisor mentions transition length unprompted, that's a BUY signal. It means they're evaluating buyers on this criterion and G&B's "customized" flexibility is a differentiator.
