When Kay introduces a hypothetical constraint during strategic discussion ("we wrap by Feb 2027," "have to acquire in 6 months," "what if we only had half the budget," "assume we can't hire anyone"), she is applying it as a pressure-test lens on the current analysis — NOT declaring it as a binding operational fact that should shape all future work.

**Why:** On 2026-04-21, during a niche-ranking conversation, Kay said "we wrap by Feb 2027. Have to acquire in 6 months." Claude interpreted this as a new durable project deadline and proposed saving it as `project_six_month_acquisition_deadline.md`. Kay corrected: "Project six months was a one off request, not a fact." The constraint served its purpose within the analysis (re-scoring niches under tighter time pressure) and did not need to persist beyond the session as a binding commitment.

**How to apply:**

1. **Default interpretation:** a constraint introduced mid-conversation is a stress-test framing unless Kay explicitly says "this is our actual deadline," "this is binding," "commit this as our plan," or uses similar durable-commitment language.

2. **Within-session use:** apply the constraint fully to the analysis at hand. Reframe recommendations, flip rankings, flag trade-offs — whatever the constraint requires. The constraint is real for this conversation.

3. **Session-end codification test:** before saving as a `project_*.md` memory or a durable context fact, ask: "did Kay explicitly commit to this as operating reality, or did she use it as a framing lens?" If the latter, DO NOT save as a durable project fact. The decision trace or session-decisions entry captures that the constraint was applied; that's enough.

4. **If unclear, ask once:** "Is the Feb 2027 wrap a binding deadline we're planning around, or a stress-test framing for this analysis?" One direct question resolves it without weeks of misalignment.

5. **This distinction matters especially for:** timelines, budgets, headcount caps, geographic constraints, scope reductions, portfolio-trim scenarios. All commonly introduced as "what if" framings that shouldn't harden into policy.
