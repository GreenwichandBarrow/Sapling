Kay has spoken directly with multiple art storage operators (ACU/Acumen partners, Hangman, Chromatic, ArtMovement, others from 2025 outreach). The unified message: **there is no shortage of demand**. Galleries, collectors, dealers, museums, family offices — all of them want more storage capacity, climate-controlled, secure, well-located. The constraint is **the business model is cost-heavy**.

**Cost-heavy means:**
- Real estate (warehouse leases or capex)
- Climate control + security infrastructure (24/7 monitoring, fire suppression, humidity control)
- Skilled labor (art handlers, registrars, condition specialists — limited talent pool)
- Insurance (high-value inventory, niche carriers)
- Transport fleet (specialized vehicles, drivers, fuel)
- Customer service (every piece is custom — intake, condition reports, transit logs, claims)

**Why this matters for G&B's thesis:**

This is the perfect AI-integration target. AI attacks the cost side, not the demand side:
- Intake automation, condition reports via computer vision, scheduling/route optimization, customer service triage, claims processing, insurance documentation — all addressable
- Demand-side AI (marketing, lead gen) is irrelevant — the demand is already there

Saltoun's "AI integration" reactivation thesis is structurally correct: cost-heavy + demand-rich + AI-attackable = ideal margin expansion play.

**Implications for searcher math:**
- Don't model revenue growth assumptions. Model margin expansion + roll-up.
- AI lift on existing revenue: 8.5% → ~12-13% margin (+4pp from labor automation)
- Roll-up scale economies: combined back-office (one CFO, one IT, one CS team) lifts another +2-4pp
- Combined: 8.5% → 16-17% margin over 5 years on consolidated revenue
- That's the path to $10M searcher returns on a $20-30M revenue platform built via 1 anchor + 2-3 tuck-ins

**Implications for outreach:**
- Lead with operations/AI/cost structure, NOT growth/marketing/demand
- Owners already know they have demand. Telling them "we'll grow revenue" sounds naive
- Telling them "we see operating leverage in your back-office and want to build a national platform that pools cost across multiple operators" sounds informed
- The Saltoun-framed AI angle reinforces this — owners will respect the diagnosis

**Implications for tuck-in selection:**
- Look for high-cost, low-margin operators with strong demand backlogs (waitlists, turnaways)
- BN's "Exceptional" rated names (Acumen, Archive, Box Art, Crozier) are anchor candidates
- Tuck-ins likely from BN's "Average" tier (Safe Art Transport, WelPak, Aiston, Maquette, Hangman, Primary Art Services, Cadogan Tate, Gander & White) where back-office consolidation has biggest lift

**Why this insight is durable:**
- Sourced from multiple operators, not a single anecdote
- Aligned with public industry data (UOVO, Crozier, Cirkers all expanding capacity, not chasing customers)
- Aligned with Saltoun's AI-margin thesis from 4/19
- Aligned with Britta Nelson's industry expert ratings methodology

**How to apply:**
- Anytime Art Storage niche math is run, default to margin/cost framework, NOT revenue growth framework
- Anytime Art Storage outreach is drafted, lead with operations/cost/AI angle, NOT growth/marketing/demand
- Pre-screen targets on demand backlog signals (waitlists, turnaways, capacity utilization >85%) — these are the ones where margin expansion has runway
- When investor or skeptic raises "is there demand?" — Kay can cite multiple operator conversations, this is settled
