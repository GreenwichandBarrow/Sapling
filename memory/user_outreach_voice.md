Kay's best-performing cold outreach email (Hangman NYC — led to 39-message thread, site visit, NDA):

> Hi David,
> Hope you're doing well and enjoying the summer. I came across Hangman NYC and was impressed by the work you've done in the art services space. I also noticed we share an NYU connection.
> I run Greenwich & Barrow, a fund focused on acquiring and growing exceptional businesses in niche art-related services. I'm currently researching the art storage and installation sector, and given your expertise, I'd welcome the chance to connect and learn more about your perspective on the market.
> If you're open, I'd be glad to find a short time to connect.
> Very best, Kay

**What worked:** The warmth, the NYU hook, the curiosity about their space, the soft ask. Led to a 39-message thread, site visit, and NDA.

**What didn't work:** "a fund focused on acquiring" — David immediately asked about money, as if Kay was PE selling at top dollar. The fund language made it transactional. Everything else was right.

**The fix (March 2026):** Same email, just drop the G&B/fund line. The Hangman email IS the template — minus that one sentence.

**Pattern:**
1. Warm opener ("Hope you're doing well")
2. "I came across {company}" + something specific that impressed you
3. A real connection point if one exists (shared school, location, mutual contact)
4. "I'm currently researching the {space}" + "given your expertise, I'd welcome the chance to connect"
5. Soft ask: "If you're open, I'd love to find a short time to connect"
6. Sign off: "Very best, Kay"

**Hard rules:**
- NEVER call G&B a "fund" or describe what G&B does — it triggers PE assumptions
- NEVER mention revenue, employee count, or financial metrics — could be wrong, and signals you care about money not legacy
- NEVER reveal thesis/growth strategy (underpenetration, consolidation opportunity, etc.)
- Don't make claims you can't source ("your name keeps coming up" — unless someone actually said it)
- No em dashes, use periods/commas/line breaks
- The email is about THEM. Kay's background lives on LinkedIn, not in the email.

**Key insight:** Kay's luxury/Chanel background means everyone checks LinkedIn before responding. The email opens the door, LinkedIn closes the credibility gap. Deep research on each owner to find the specific connection point is where Claude adds the most value.

**Anti-patterns:** Generic templates, high-volume spray, mentioning financials, calling G&B a fund, leading with Kay's story instead of theirs, anything that sounds like an overseas VA wrote it.

## Branding Session (April 5, 2026)

Kay's story beats for Variant B (direct):
- Daughter of a serial entrepreneur, grew up in and around small business
- Went into fashion/luxury because she loved the mix of art and commerce
- A decade at Chanel, recognized the client crossover in the art world
- Looking to acquire one business and run it herself, full time
- Not PE, not a fund, not a portfolio — one business, full attention

**Key lines (use verbatim, don't paraphrase):**
- "After a decade in the luxury world, I recognized the client crossover in the art world"
- "I grew up around small business through my father, a serial entrepreneur"
- "went into luxury because I loved the mix of art and commerce"
- "I'm looking to acquire one business and run it myself, full time"

**What NOT to include in Day 0:**
- Don't tell them you want to handle business ops (implies you're not client-facing)
- Don't mention PE even to say you're not PE (introduces a comparison they weren't making)
- Don't over-research them — keep target references light ("impressed by the work you've done")
- Don't say "I knew this was where I wanted to be" — sounds phony
- Don't say "stepped away" from Chanel — could imply being let go

**Inspired by Kevin Hong (Caprae) branding framework:**
- Why I'm writing → Who I am → The ask (presentation format)
- Every sentence must add independent value
- Customization early and clear
- Be bold, personable, stand out
