## Kay's canonical phrases (use these by default)

| Slot | Kay's wording | Don't use |
|---|---|---|
| Opener | **"Hope this finds you well."** | "Hope you're doing well" (close but slightly less Kay-voice) |
| Sign-off line | **"Looking forward to hearing from you"** | "Thanks!" (too casual for first-touch) |
| Closer | **"All the best,"** | "Best," (Kay uses the longer form by default; "Best," only when extra brief) |
| Address | **"Hi {Name},"** + line break | "Hi {Name}!" (exclamation is too informal for first-touch) |

## Humility / deference pattern (mandatory for asks to higher-tenure peers)

When reaching out to someone with significantly more experience or operating tenure (e.g., a 10+ year operator from her perspective as a current searcher), Kay layers in explicit deference:

- **"I'm sure you are very busy and I would appreciate any time you can spare."**
- **"Would you be free to connect?"** (open-ended, no timeframe constraint — let them pick)

This is NOT optional. The deference layer reads as gracious, not weak. It's a Kay-voice signature for outreach where she's the one asking.

## Compliment + congrats pattern

When mentioning the recipient's accomplishment or what someone said about them, Kay adds **"- congrats"** or **"congrats on [X]"** explicitly. Example: "A friend mentioned your name and that had a great experience in the space - congrats."

The dash-congrats construction is a Kay signature — short, warm, acknowledging.

## Vocabulary preferences

- **"Traditional search"** — use this term when describing her search model (search-fund vocabulary, recognizable to fellow searchers and ecosystem people). Better than "running a search" alone.
- **"Based in NY"** — preferred over "NYC", "New York", or "based out of NYC"
- **"Happy to come your way for coffee"** — the canonical travel-offer phrase (concrete, warm, deferential)
- **"Grew up on the North Shore of LI"** — personal hook used when geographically relevant; she drops "Long Island" → "LI" for brevity

## Friend reference pattern

When someone mentioned the recipient to her but the friend is NOT close and didn't offer a warm intro, Kay STILL references "A friend mentioned your name" without naming the friend. The pattern:
- ✅ "A friend mentioned your name and that [you] had a great experience in the space"
- ❌ "{Friend's name} mentioned you"
- ❌ Omitting the friend reference entirely (loses warmth and the soft social-proof signal)

The DON'T-name-the-friend rule is about discretion, not deletion.

## Confirmation-reply pattern

When the reply IS a confirmation (annual review, financial close-out, RSVP confirm, doc receipt), Kay uses a tighter pattern than first-touch outreach:

- **Opener**: `"Hope this finds you well, and happy to confirm."` — combines the canonical opener with the action verb in one breath. NOT `"thank you for sending this over"` which adds a thank-you that isn't core to the action.
- **Confirmation line**: Drop `"can confirm that"` filler. Say `"the information you provided ties out cleanly"` not `"I can confirm that the information below ties out cleanly"`. The next clause IS the confirmation; preamble is noise.
- **Offer-to-help**: ONE line max, don't double up. `"Happy to provide any backup documentation [counterparty's accountants/team] may need to complete their review."` is enough. Don't follow with `"please let me know if there's anything else I can pull together"` — same offer, duplicative.
- **Closer**: When the thread is closing (no further reply expected), DROP `"Looking forward to hearing from you"`. It's a Kay-canonical closer for warm threads where a reply is expected, but on confirmation replies it implies a back-and-forth that may not happen.
- **Multi-stakeholder sign-off**: When the reply has multiple recipients (Cc'd accountants, team, etc.), use `"All the best to you and the team,"` instead of plain `"All the best,"`. The `"to you and the team"` extension folds warmth toward ALL recipients into the sign-off line itself, eliminating the need for a separate `"please give my best to [principal]"` sentence.

## Form-factor

For first-touch LinkedIn outreach to a 3rd-degree connection:
- Kay's preferred path is **the full message (InMail length / message-after-connect length), NOT the 300-char Connect-request note**.
- Don't constrain to 300 chars by default; offer her both versions but lead with the full longer message as primary.

## Firm-name mention

In first-touch peer-to-peer outreach where the recipient doesn't need it yet, Kay sometimes OMITS the "Greenwich & Barrow" firm name and just says "running a traditional search." The firm name is contextual, not first-touch essential. Don't force it in if the message reads cleaner without.

## Precipitating trace

I'd love to hear from someone who's been through the search and is now operating. I'm sure you are very busy and I would appreciate any time you can spare. Would you be free to connect? I grew up on the North Shore of LI and happy to come your way for coffee.
> Looking forward to hearing from you
> All the best,
> Kay

My iterations had: "Hi Kevin!" (too informal), "Hope you're doing well" (close but not Kay-voice), "Free next week or two? Thanks!" (too casual, no deference), missed "I'm sure you are very busy" entirely. Captured all the corrections here.
